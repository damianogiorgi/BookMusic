The morning kitchen was full of light. Sunbeams stretched across the wooden floor, and a small kitten chased a runaway ball of yarn beneath the table, batting it from paw to paw with delighted, clumsy little leaps.

By afternoon the sky had turned to slate. Rain came soft against the windows, and she sat very still in the dim room, watching the grey water run down the glass, thinking of things she could not change.

That night she dreamed of a silver world on the far side of the mirror. Everything shimmered and bent; the floor was made of moving light, and the ordinary rules of waking life quietly dissolved into something stranger and more beautiful.

Then, without warning, a hush fell over the great hall. The candles guttered. Somewhere beyond the heavy doors something moved that should not have moved, and every person in the room felt the cold certainty of danger creeping closer.

When dawn finally broke, the danger had passed. Birds returned to the eaves, the air warmed, and a slow, simple calm settled over the house like a held breath finally, gently let go.
